About Me

I am a senior at the University of Michigan graduating in April 2023 with a bachelor's in computer science. Ever since I can remember technology has always fascinated me. I think my earliest interaction with computer science is an early robotics course in maybe third grade where we used these coding blocks to program a lego robot. Those things were cool and I really wanted to buy one for my own. However, it wasn't until a career fair in middle school, where a software engineer from Google came by and explained how he'd help implement the biography feature that appears when you search someone famous on Google. This blew my mind as everyone uses this amazing feature, but do not realize how useful it truly is. Now everytime I look up the a movie or celebrity I think of this moment where someone nobody knows made something that changed everyone's lives. After that, I knew exactly what I wanted to do and I laid out a basic plan.

I rigorously studied the Specialized High School Admissions Test (SHSAT for short) to get into arguably the best highschool in New York, and thanks to the preparation Bronx Science gave me, I managed to get accepted to the University of Michigan. All the while, I was only able to take maybe 2 classes in middle school and high school combined which taught me the basics of python and coding in general. In order to get a bit more familiar with code, I decided to take a research opportunity at the City College of New York (CCNY) in association with the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA). This experience fine tuned some of my coding skills and gave me experience doing hands on research which I presented at a symposium. Using some python and GIS along with an air pollution sensor, we were able to collect air quality in Times Square and the World Trade Center, though we did unofficially check near the CCNY campus. By collecting the air quality data at various places and at various times, we were able to come to multiple conclusions including a general trend towards higher concentration of air pollution until the evening. Unofficial findings made us speculate that factors of economic quality of the areas we tested had a lot to do with the air quality as the poorer nearby Harlem area of CCNY had higher air pollution levels then its more wealthy counter parts like Times Square. Since the program lasted only the duration of one summer, we did not have time to explore this possibility further but nonetheless, it was an intriguing and amazing experience I am glad to have been apart of.

I was so intrigued with research that I decided to participate in yet another research opportunity as a freshman at U of M. This time I was in charge of transferring countless logs of college sports financial data from various college teams. At the end of the research which maybe accumulated to about 80 hours of research, our research team developed an elaborate database providing an impressive resource for college sports finance research. During my symposium, I was able to present some interesting findings including revenue disparities between men's and women's teams which seemed to correlate with funding disparities both teams received. Ultimately, while nothing concrete could be asserted from a surface level investigation, the database provided a phenomenal resource for not just answering some questions, but discovering new questions that are worth addressing. Since the end of my research I have been focused on my studies which most recently landed me on the Dean's Honor list during the Winter 2022 semester, and I am steady on my way to graduating and joining the job force. This semester I have truly honed in my skills in my most demanding and productive courses yet and the product of my work can be found in the projects section of this website.
